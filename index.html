Phase 01 — The Attack Chain

How This Attack
Unfolded

Phishing attacks follow a predictable kill chain. Understanding each phase helps you recognize and stop them before they succeed.

📧

1. Lure Delivery

An email, SMS, or social media message is sent with an urgent or enticing message — "Your account was compromised, click here immediately." The goal is to trigger panic or curiosity.

🔗

2. Malicious Link

The link directs you to a cloned website that looks identical to a legitimate service like Google, a bank, or Microsoft. The URL may be slightly different — e.g., g00gle-login.com instead of google.com.

🖥️

3. Credential Harvest

You enter your username and password. The fake site captures this data and sends it to the attacker's server — often in real time. You may not even realize anything is wrong.

🚪

4. Account Takeover

With your credentials, attackers log in to your real account, change the password to lock you out, steal data, send spam, or pivot deeper into your organization's network.

Phase 02 — Spot the Threat

Red Flags You
Should Have Noticed

Every phishing page has tells. Train your eyes to catch these warning signs before it's too late.

🌐

Suspicious URL

The real Google login is at accounts.google.com. Look for misspellings, extra words, or wrong domains before entering any credentials.

🔒

Missing or Fake HTTPS

A padlock icon doesn't guarantee safety — attackers get free SSL certificates too. Always verify the domain name, not just the lock icon.

⚡

Urgency & Pressure

"Your account will be deleted in 24 hours!" Urgency is a manipulation tactic. Legitimate services don't threaten you into clicking immediately.

📋

Unexpected Request

Did you initiate this login? If you followed a link from an email you didn't expect, go directly to the website instead of clicking through.

✉️

Sender Email Mismatch

The display name may say "Google Security" but hover over it — the actual address might be noreply@someweirdsite.xyz.

🖼️

Poor Design or Broken Images

Cloned pages often have missing logos, broken layouts, or slightly off fonts. Compare carefully against the real site you know.

Phase 04 — Your Defense

What To Do If
You're Phished

If you believe you've entered credentials on a phishing site in real life, act fast. Every minute matters.

01

Change Your Password Immediately

Go directly to the real website (type it in manually) and change your password right away. Do not click any links from the same email.

02

Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even if attackers have your password, 2FA prevents them from logging in. Use an authenticator app — not SMS, which can be intercepted.

03

Report to Your IT / Security Team

In an organization, notify your IT department immediately. They can isolate the threat, revoke sessions, and warn other employees.

04

Check for Unauthorized Activity

Review your account's login history and active sessions. Revoke access for any sessions you don't recognize.

05

Check Other Accounts Using the Same Password

If you reuse passwords, attackers will try them elsewhere. Update all accounts that share that password immediately.
